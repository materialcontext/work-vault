Why Leave? Why Oregon?

Push and Pull Factors of Migration

‚öñÔ∏è The Economic and Social Forces

üî¥ Push Factors: Why Leave the East?

  • Economic Depression: Panic of 1837, farm failures, unemployment
  • Land Shortage: Eastern farmland expensive, inheritance challenges
  • Overcrowding: Cities becoming crowded, competition intense
  • Disease: Cholera outbreaks in eastern cities
  • Debt: Many farmers in financial trouble

üü¢ Pull Factors: Why Oregon?

  • Free Land: 640 acres per family under Donation Land Act
  • Fertile Soil: Reports of rich Willamette Valley farmland
  • Mild Climate: No harsh winters, year-round growing season
  • Adventure: Frontier spirit, new opportunities
  • Religious Freedom: Missionaries promoted Oregon settlement

üìú Oregon Trail Diary Entry

"Started from Independence with high hopes and considerable anxiety. Have never been so far from home. Left behind a good farm but it was getting too crowded, and the stories of Oregon's fertile valleys and mild climate were too tempting to resist. If we can just make it through the mountains before winter, we'll have 640 acres of the finest land in the world. Worth the risk for our children's future."
‚Äî Samuel Hancock, Missouri farmer, April 15, 1845

Life on the Trail

Daily Reality of the 2,000-Mile Journey

üìñ Primary Source: Trail Diary Entries

May 20, 1847: "Crossed the Platte today. Lost our best ox in the quicksand. Children are getting sick from the alkali water. Beginning to wonder if we made a terrible mistake. But turning back now would mean we've lost everything for nothing."

July 4, 1847: "Celebrated Independence Day at Fort Laramie. Met traders who say we're making good time, but the hardest part is still ahead. Had to abandon Mother's dresser - too heavy for the oxen. Every mile costs us something precious."

September 12, 1847: "Finally made it to Oregon City! Five months and 18 days from Independence. Lost two oxen, most of our furniture, and nearly lost little Mary to fever. But we made it. The land here is everything they said - rich, black soil as far as you can see."
‚Äî Martha Reed, Ohio to Oregon, 1847

The Other Side of the Story

Impact on Native American Communities

üåç Different Perspectives on the Same Events

üë®‚Äçüë©‚Äçüëß‚Äçüë¶ For American Migrants

  • Opportunity: Free land, new start, better life for children
  • Adventure: Frontier spirit, exploration, independence
  • Economic Freedom: Escape debt, crowding, limited opportunities
  • Success Stories: Many found prosperity in Oregon Territory
  • Community Building: Established towns, farms, businesses

üèïÔ∏è For Native American Communities

  • Disruption: Traditional hunting grounds damaged by wagon traffic
  • Disease: Cholera, smallpox spread by migrants killed thousands
  • Resource Depletion: Grass eaten by oxen, game scared away
  • Environmental Damage: Trails, campsites, pollution of water sources
  • Cultural Impact: Sacred sites disturbed, traditional ways threatened

üì∞ Native American Perspective

"The white people have made a road through our land. They kill our buffalo and leave the meat to rot. They frighten away the elk and deer. Their oxen eat all the grass our horses need. When they camp by our streams, they leave sickness behind them. Many of our people have died from the spotted sickness. They say they only want to pass through, but more and more keep coming every year."
‚Äî Lakota oral history account of Oregon Trail impact

Modern Connections

From Oregon Trail to Today

üîó Migration Then and Now

The Oregon Trail represented economic migration - people moving for better opportunities. How does this compare to migration patterns today?

üíº Economic Migration

üåç Environmental Impact

Then: Wagon trains altered landscapes, depleted resources along trail

üèòÔ∏è Community Response
