üìñ "My Name" by Sandra Cisneros

Author Context

Sandra Cisneros is a Mexican-American author who writes about growing up in Chicago. This story is told by a young narrator named Esperanza.

As We Read, Think About:

How does Esperanza's experience with her name connect to your own name story?

First Reading

Read aloud together, then discuss initial impressions

üîç Close Reading Discussion

Heritage and Legacy

Question: What does Esperanza inherit from her great-grandmother besides the name?

Look for: both strong-willed, great-grandmother's confinement, the window imagery

Language and Sound

Question: How does her name sound different in English versus Spanish? Find the exact descriptions.

Look for: "muddy color" vs. "silver," "thick records" vs. "soft like tissue paper"

Identity and Choice

Question: What names does she want instead? What does this tell us about who she wants to become?

Look for: her list of alternative names, the desire to "baptize myself"

Key Insight: "Why does the same name feel completely different depending on the language and culture?"

üåç Global Cultural Connections

Naming Practices Worldwide

  • Iceland: Last names = father's name + son/daughter
  • China: Family name first, given name reflects hopes
  • Hispanic cultures: Multiple family names preserve both sides
  • Many African cultures: Names reflect birth circumstances or family dreams

Analysis Question

What patterns do you see in how names connect to family, culture, and identity across different societies?
