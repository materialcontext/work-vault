üó∫Ô∏è Columbus: Economic Problem-Solver, Not Explorer

Columbus, Letter to Ferdinand and Isabella (1493):
"...the lands are very fertile, and filled with an infinite number of gold mines and precious stones, and spices... Your Highnesses will command cities and fortresses to be built, and the country subdued, and Your Highnesses will thus have vast states and dominions..."

Columbus's Specific Solutions

  • Gold extraction: Direct access without Portuguese middlemen
  • Territorial control: Land-based empire using Spanish military strengths
  • Agricultural production: New crops and tribute systems
  • Strategic position: Atlantic routes bypassing Ottoman control

Why "Cities and Fortresses"?

  • Territorial approach matches Spanish capabilities
  • Uses expensive army for productive purposes
  • Creates permanent revenue sources
  • Provides administrative control
How does Columbus's territorial approach address Spanish capabilities and constraints differently than Portuguese commercial solutions?

üìú Encomienda: Revolutionary Political-Economic Innovation

Laws of the Indies (1542):
"We entrust to you [encomendero] the Indians of the village of X, so that you may teach them in matters of our holy Catholic faith and protect them... and they shall serve you with their persons and labor in your mines, farms, and other enterprises..."

üîÑ Revolutionary Features

  • Hybrid sovereignty: Combines European and indigenous political structures
  • Private-public partnership: Crown grants authority, colonists provide administration
  • Economic diversification: Agriculture, mining, and tribute collection
  • Cultural integration: Spanish legal systems applied to indigenous populations
How does encomienda solve Spanish problems of territorial control, revenue generation, and administrative costs simultaneously?
