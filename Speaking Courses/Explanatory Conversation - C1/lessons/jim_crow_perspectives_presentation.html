📚 The Rise of Jim Crow (1877-1920s)

1877: Compromise Ends Reconstruction

Federal troops withdrawn from South, white Democrats regain power

1890s: Legal Segregation Begins

Southern states pass Jim Crow laws, disenfranchisement

1896: Plessy v. Ferguson

"Separate but equal" becomes legal doctrine

📊 The System's Impact

  • Mississippi: 147,000 → 9,000 Black voters
  • Louisiana: 130,000 → 1,342 Black voters
  • Lynching: 80-160 African Americans annually
  • Segregation: All public facilities separated

Key Question

How did people facing the same oppressive system choose such different strategies to resist it?

🏛️ White Southern Perspective: "Restoring Order"

How White Southerners Justified Jim Crow

Same laws, completely different reasoning

🛡️ "Protection" Arguments

"Allowing black people to attend white schools would mean constantly subjecting them to adverse feeling and opinion."
- Early 20th-century scholar
  • Claimed segregation protected African Americans
  • Argued integration would cause "race consciousness"
  • Portrayed separation as beneficial to both races

📊 Political and Economic Motivations

  • Depression-era whites feared job competition
  • Politicians used racism to win poor white votes
  • "Grandfather clauses" split poor whites from Blacks
  • Newspapers exaggerated Black crimes

🤝 vs ✊ African American Strategy Debate

📚 Booker T. Washington: "Atlanta Compromise"
"In all things purely social we can be as separate as the five fingers, and yet one as the hand in all things essential to mutual progress."
- Atlanta Speech, 1895
  • Strategy: Accept segregation temporarily
  • Focus: Vocational education, economic progress
  • Reasoning: Economic success → eventual political rights
  • Risk: White supremacists used his ideas against Black rights
⚖️ W.E.B. Du Bois: Immediate Rights
"Racism would not allow black people to rise out of poverty in the first place."
- Critique of Washington
  • Strategy: Demand civil rights immediately
  • Focus: Higher education, "Talented Tenth"
  • Reasoning: Political power needed first
  • Action: Co-founded NAACP (1909)

Key Insight

Both leaders wanted equality but completely disagreed on how to achieve it. Their different life experiences shaped their strategies.

👩‍⚖️ Women's Resistance: Ida B. Wells

Fighting Multiple Battles

Racism AND sexism simultaneously

🚂 Personal Experience Shapes Activism

"When the conductor tried to drag her from her seat, she bit his hand. Two men helped forcibly remove Ida while white passengers applauded."
- 1884 Train Incident
  • Bought first-class ticket, forced to move
  • Physical resistance to segregation
  • Sued railroad and won (temporarily)

📰 Anti-Lynching Crusade Strategy

"To arouse the conscience of America"
- Purpose of "Southern Horrors"
  • Used journalism to expose lynching reality
  • Forced into exile after editorial
  • International speaking tours
  • Combined anti-racism with women's suffrage

🏘️ Community-Based Resistance Strategies

🎭 "Walking the Tightrope"

"Protective socialization by black people themselves was created to accommodate white-imposed sanctions while subtly encouraging challenges to those sanctions."
- Historian William Chafe
  • Appear to accept segregation publicly
  • Build strength within Black community
  • Prepare for future challenges
  • Churches as organizing centers

🏃‍♂️ The Great Migration Strategy

  • Individual/family resistance through movement
  • Chicago Defender encouraged Northern migration
  • White Southerners tried to ban the newspaper
  • Migration as "voting with your feet"
  • Economic impact on Southern labor

Strategy Insight

People facing oppression don't just choose between accommodation and confrontation—they create diverse, creative forms of resistance.

🏙️ White Northern Perspective: Indifference and Complicity

🏛️ Federal Government Abandonment
"Government abandoned the cause of black equality. In white America, the belief in white supremacy and black inferiority deepened."
  • No federal intervention to stop Jim Crow
  • Supreme Court legitimized segregation
  • Congress blocked anti-lynching legislation
🏭 Northern Segregation Reality
  • Property requirements for voting
  • Segregated schools and neighborhoods
  • "Whites Only" business signs
  • Economic discrimination in employment
  • Different methods, similar goals

Key Realization

Jim Crow wasn't just a Southern problem—it was a national system of racial oppression with regional variations.

⚠️ Violence as Social Control Strategy

Lynching: The Ultimate Enforcement Tool

How terror shaped all other resistance strategies

📊 The Reality of Lynching

  • 80-160 African Americans lynched annually
  • Public spectacles with large crowds
  • Often for trivial "offenses"
  • Psychological weapon against entire community

🎯 Multiple Targets and Messages

  • Primary message: Control African American men
  • Secondary message: Control white women's choices
  • Economic message: Maintain cheap labor
  • Political message: Prevent voting and organizing

Strategic Impact

Understanding the threat of violence helps explain why African Americans chose such different resistance strategies—each had to balance effectiveness with survival.
